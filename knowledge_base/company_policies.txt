POLICY 1: HIGH PRIORITY SHIPMENT ESCALATION
If a shipment is marked High priority and has a Medium or High risk level, it must be reviewed before normal execution continues.
High priority shipments may affect customer commitments, production schedules, or service-level agreements.
Recommended actions include closer monitoring, customer communication, and escalation to logistics leadership.

POLICY 2: DELAYED SHIPMENT RESPONSE
If a shipment is already delayed, the logistics team must review the cause of delay and determine whether the shipment should continue, be rerouted, or be held.
Delayed shipments with additional route, weather, or news disruption risk require active monitoring.
If the risk level is High, the shipment should not be automatically approved without review.

POLICY 3: TEMPERATURE-SENSITIVE OR SENSITIVE CARGO HANDLING
Shipments involving pharmaceuticals, perishable food, medical supplies, or other sensitive cargo require additional handling control.
If weather conditions include extreme heat, freezing temperatures, storms, or route delays, the shipment should receive elevated risk attention.
Sensitive cargo may require alternate routing, temperature monitoring, or carrier confirmation before release.

POLICY 4: WEATHER DISRUPTION RESPONSE
If severe weather is detected at the origin, destination, or along the route, shipment execution should be reviewed.
Severe weather includes heavy rain, snow, hurricanes, flooding, tornado warnings, or extreme heat.
Recommended actions include checking carrier availability, route conditions, and estimated delivery impact.

POLICY 5: ROUTE RISK AND TRAFFIC DELAY RESPONSE
If route analysis shows Medium risk, the shipment may continue with monitoring.
If route analysis shows High risk or traffic delay is significant, the logistics team should consider rerouting, delaying dispatch, or confirming the route with the carrier.
Long-distance shipments should receive additional review when combined with weather or external disruption risk.

POLICY 6: EXTERNAL NEWS DISRUPTION RESPONSE
If external news intelligence identifies logistics disruption, road closure, strike, congestion, wildfire, port delay, or supply chain disruption near the shipment route, the shipment should be reviewed.
A single weak article should not automatically block shipment execution.
Multiple relevant disruption signals should increase the risk level and may require rerouting or delay.

POLICY 7: AUTO-APPROVAL RULE
Low risk shipments may be automatically approved.
Medium risk shipments may be automatically approved only if there is no active delay, no severe weather, and no high route risk.
High risk shipments require human review before final approval.

POLICY 8: REROUTING GUIDELINE
Rerouting should be considered when route risk is High, traffic delay is significant, severe weather affects the route, or external disruption signals suggest transportation instability.
Rerouting is preferred over delaying when the shipment is high priority or time-sensitive and an alternate route is practical.

POLICY 9: DELAY RECOMMENDATION GUIDELINE
Delay is recommended when conditions make shipment continuation unreliable or unsafe.
Delay may be appropriate when severe weather, route closure, or active disruption is likely to cause failed delivery.
Delay should be considered for sensitive cargo if temperature or handling conditions cannot be confirmed.

POLICY 10: SHIP RECOMMENDATION GUIDELINE
Ship is recommended when risk is Low or controlled Medium, route conditions are stable, weather is acceptable, and no major disruption intelligence is detected.
Medium risk shipments may still ship if the risk is explainable and manageable through monitoring.
